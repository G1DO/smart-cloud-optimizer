\begin{abstract}
Enterprise cloud spending continues to grow, yet a large fraction is wasted on
over-provisioned and mispriced resources. The difficulty is sharpest when a
steady base demand is punctuated by \emph{intermittent random demand surges},
which force operators either to over-reserve capacity year-round or to absorb
spikes at expensive on-demand rates. We present \textbf{Smart Cloud Optimizer},
an end-to-end system that couples time-series demand forecasting with
optimization-based capacity reservation to minimize cloud cost while preserving
headroom for surges. The system (i)~collects cost and utilization telemetry from
Amazon Web Services, (ii)~forecasts daily demand using an ensemble of five
models with anomaly-aware preprocessing, (iii)~separates demand into a
\emph{base} component, served by committed reserved instances, and a
\emph{surge} component, served by elastic on-demand headroom sized at the
$95^{\text{th}}$ percentile of load, and (iv)~solves a mixed-integer linear
program for right-sizing complemented by eight cost-reduction rules. On a
twelve-month workload derived from open production traces, the forecasting
ensemble attains a mean absolute percentage error of $7.9\%$ at a one-week
horizon, and the optimizer identifies \$590.40 of recoverable monthly cost
($\approx 26\%$ of the bill) across 19 recommendations. The results show that
combining short-horizon forecasting with commitment-based reservation yields
substantial, automated, and explainable savings.
\end{abstract}

\section{Introduction}
Worldwide public-cloud spending is projected to approach \$600 billion
annually~\cite{gartner2023cloud}, and industry surveys consistently report that
organizations waste roughly a quarter to a third of that spend on idle or
oversized resources~\cite{flexera2024}. The root cause is a mismatch between
\emph{provisioned} capacity and \emph{actual} demand. Cloud providers offer
several pricing modes to manage this mismatch: \emph{on-demand} instances, which
are flexible but expensive; \emph{reserved} instances (one- or three-year
commitments), which are substantially cheaper but rigid; and \emph{spot}
instances, which are cheap but interruptible~\cite{awspricing}.

Choosing among these modes is straightforward when demand is stationary, but
real workloads rarely are. A common and costly pattern is a steady
\emph{base} demand overlaid with \emph{intermittent random surges}---marketing
campaigns, product launches, seasonal traffic, or batch jobs---whose timing or
magnitude is uncertain. Provisioning enough reserved capacity to cover the
surges wastes money during the long quiet periods; provisioning only for the
base and absorbing surges on-demand pays a premium precisely when usage is
highest. Recent operations-research work formalizes this tension as a capacity
reservation problem in which a firm meets base demand with standard
\emph{base contracts} and copes with surges through flexible
\emph{supplementary contracts}, deriving newsvendor-type optimal reservation
levels and a threshold purchase/cancel policy~\cite{chen2024cost}.

This paper takes a complementary, \emph{systems-and-data} view of the same
problem. Rather than assume the demand distribution is known, we \emph{learn} it
from telemetry and drive reservation and right-sizing decisions from the
resulting forecast. We describe Smart Cloud Optimizer, a deployed platform that
turns raw Amazon Web Services (AWS) billing and CloudWatch data into ranked,
explainable cost-saving actions. Its central idea mirrors the
base/supplementary decomposition of~\cite{chen2024cost}: committed reserved
instances serve the forecasted base demand, while elastic capacity sized with a
percentile-plus-headroom rule absorbs surges, and a mixed-integer program
selects the cheapest feasible instance configuration.

\noindent\textbf{Contributions.} This paper makes the following contributions:
\begin{itemize}
\item A three-stage architecture (\emph{collect--analyze--optimize}) that
integrates multi-service AWS telemetry collection, demand forecasting, and
optimization in a single multi-tenant system (Section~\ref{sec:system}).
\item A cost model that decomposes demand into a base component served by
reserved commitments and a surge component served by on-demand headroom, and a
mixed-integer linear program (MILP) for right-sizing under this model
(Section~\ref{sec:formulation}).
\item An anomaly-aware forecasting ensemble of five models, selected by
walk-forward cross-validation, together with a rolling-statistics surge
detector (Section~\ref{sec:method}).
\item An empirical evaluation on a twelve-month workload derived from open
production traces, reporting forecasting accuracy and a measured \$590.40
monthly saving ($\approx 26\%$) with full breakdown (Section~\ref{sec:eval}).
\end{itemize}

\section{Related Work}
\label{sec:related}

\textbf{Capacity reservation as an optimization problem.}
The economics of reserved versus on-demand capacity have been studied
extensively in operations research. Chen \emph{et~al.}~\cite{chen2024cost} model
intermittent random demand surges and characterize an optimal newsvendor
reservation level plus a two-threshold contract-management policy; Wang
\emph{et~al.}~\cite{wang2014rightsizing} study cloud brokerage that reserves in
bulk and resells, and Mitrani~\cite{mitrani2014reserved} optimizes server-farm
provisioning under power and performance constraints. These works assume the
demand distribution is given analytically. Our system instead estimates demand
empirically from telemetry and feeds the estimate into the reservation
decision, trading closed-form optimality for deployability on real accounts.

\textbf{Cloud demand and workload forecasting.}
Predicting cloud workloads is a mature area. Calheiros
\emph{et~al.}~\cite{calheiros2015workload} apply ARIMA to workload prediction and
quantify the QoS impact; Khan \emph{et~al.}~\cite{khan2012workload} use multiple
time-series models for co-clustered workloads; and Roy
\emph{et~al.}~\cite{roy2011autoscaling} drive predictive autoscaling from
forecasts. Large-provider studies such as Resource
Central~\cite{cortez2017azure} predict VM behavior at datacenter scale. We adopt
established forecasting models (Holt-Winters/ETS~\cite{hyndman2021fpp},
SARIMA~\cite{box2015timeseries}, and Prophet~\cite{taylor2018prophet}) but target
the \emph{cost} signal directly and use the forecast for reservation sizing
rather than autoscaling.

\textbf{Right-sizing, anomaly detection, and FinOps.}
Auto-scaling and right-sizing techniques are surveyed
in~\cite{lorido2014autoscaling}. The emerging FinOps
discipline~\cite{forsgren2020finops} emphasizes continuous, accountable cost
management. Anomaly detection on cloud metrics is benchmarked by the Numenta
Anomaly Benchmark~\cite{lavin2015nab}. Our contribution is not a new forecasting
or detection algorithm but a system that \emph{composes} forecasting, anomaly
filtering, and mixed-integer optimization into an explainable cost-reduction
pipeline aligned with the base/supplementary reservation framing
of~\cite{chen2024cost}.

\section{System Model and Architecture}
\label{sec:system}

Smart Cloud Optimizer follows a three-stage pipeline---\emph{collect},
\emph{analyze}, and \emph{optimize}---over a single persistent store
(Fig.~\ref{fig:arch}). The design favors a single SQLite database (ACID,
\texttt{user\_id}-isolated multi-tenancy, upsert-based deduplication) so that
every stage reads and writes through one typed gateway.

\begin{figure*}[t]
\centering
\includegraphics[width=0.92\textwidth]{architecture.pdf}
\caption{Three-stage architecture. A collector assumes a cross-account IAM role
and pulls twelve months of cost and utilization data into a single store; the
forecasting and optimization engines read from it, and a dashboard presents
ranked recommendations.}
\label{fig:arch}
\end{figure*}

\subsection{Data Collection}
A collector pulls data from AWS Cost Explorer, CloudWatch, and the Pricing API,
plus per-service inventory for EC2, RDS, S3, Lambda, DynamoDB, EBS,
ElastiCache, ECS, ELB, and NAT gateways. Collection proceeds month-by-month over
the trailing twelve months to respect API time-window limits. In production
(``real mode'') the collector assumes a customer-provided cross-account IAM role
via STS; only the role ARN is stored, never long-lived secrets. A ``demo mode''
serves pre-loaded data for evaluation without credentials. Telemetry is
normalized into 30 relational tables keyed by \texttt{(timestamp, user\_id,
resource\_id)}, with the daily total-cost series serving as the primary
forecasting target.

\subsection{Demand Model}
\label{sec:demandmodel}
Let $d_t$ denote the realized demand (here, daily cost) on day $t$. We model it
as the sum of a slowly varying base component, a deterministic weekly seasonal
component, and an intermittent surge component:
\begin{equation}
d_t = \underbrace{b_t}_{\text{base}} + \underbrace{s_t}_{\text{seasonal}}
      + \underbrace{u_t}_{\text{surge}} + \varepsilon_t,
\label{eq:demand}
\end{equation}
where $u_t$ is zero on most days and takes large positive values during surges,
and $\varepsilon_t$ is noise. The base and seasonal parts are predictable and
should be served by cheap committed capacity; the surge part is, by
construction, hard to predict in timing and magnitude and must be served by
flexible capacity held in reserve as \emph{headroom}. This decomposition is the
data-driven analogue of the base/supplementary contract split
of~\cite{chen2024cost}.

\section{Problem Formulation}
\label{sec:formulation}

\subsection{Cost Objective}
Consider a fleet of resources $i \in \mathcal{I}$ (e.g., EC2 and RDS instances).
Each resource can be assigned a configuration $c$ from a catalog
$\mathcal{C}_i$ of candidate instance types, each with an hourly on-demand price
$p^{\text{od}}_c$ and, where eligible, a reserved price $p^{\text{ri}}_c <
p^{\text{od}}_c$. Let $H$ be the monthly hour count. We minimize the total
monthly cost subject to meeting demand with surge headroom. Writing the binary
assignment variable $x_{i,c}\in\{0,1\}$,
\begin{align}
\min_{x}\quad & \sum_{i\in\mathcal{I}}\sum_{c\in\mathcal{C}_i}
   H \, \pi_{i,c}\, x_{i,c} \label{eq:obj}\\
\text{s.t.}\quad
 & \sum_{c\in\mathcal{C}_i} x_{i,c}=1, && \forall i, \label{eq:assign}\\
 & \sum_{c\in\mathcal{C}_i} \text{vcpu}_c\, x_{i,c} \ge \rho^{\text{cpu}}_i,
   && \forall i, \label{eq:cpu}\\
 & \sum_{c\in\mathcal{C}_i} \text{mem}_c\, x_{i,c} \ge \rho^{\text{mem}}_i,
   && \forall i, \label{eq:mem}\\
 & \sum_{i}\sum_{c} H\,\pi_{i,c}\, x_{i,c} \le B, \label{eq:budget}
\end{align}
where $\pi_{i,c}=p^{\text{ri}}_c$ if resource $i$ qualifies for a reservation
(steady, long-running) and $p^{\text{od}}_c$ otherwise, and $B$ is an optional
budget cap. Constraint~\eqref{eq:assign} assigns exactly one type per resource;
\eqref{eq:cpu}--\eqref{eq:mem} enforce sufficient capacity.

\subsection{Surge-Aware Demand Sizing}
The right-hand sides $\rho^{\text{cpu}}_i$ and $\rho^{\text{mem}}_i$ encode the
surge headroom. Rather than sizing to the average (which hides spikes) or the
maximum (a single outlier), we size to the $95^{\text{th}}$ percentile of
observed utilization scaled by a headroom factor $\gamma$:
\begin{equation}
\rho^{\text{cpu}}_i = \frac{P_{95}\!\left(\text{CPU}_i\right)}{100}\cdot
   \text{vcpu}^{\text{cur}}_i \cdot \gamma, \qquad \gamma = 1.3 .
\label{eq:p95}
\end{equation}
The factor $\gamma=1.3$ reserves $30\%$ of capacity as surge headroom, the
data-driven counterpart of a supplementary reservation: enough elastic capacity
to absorb the surge term $u_t$ in~\eqref{eq:demand} without paying for it
year-round. Table~\ref{tab:notation} summarizes the notation.

\begin{table}[t]
\caption{Notation}
\label{tab:notation}
\centering
\renewcommand{\arraystretch}{1.2}
\begin{tabular}{@{}ll@{}}
\toprule
Symbol & Meaning \\
\midrule
$d_t$ & realized demand (daily cost) on day $t$ \\
$b_t,\,s_t,\,u_t$ & base, seasonal, surge components \\
$x_{i,c}$ & assign config.\ $c$ to resource $i$ (binary) \\

The MILP~\eqref{eq:obj}--\eqref{eq:budget} is solved per service with the CBC
solver via PuLP~\cite{forecastpkg}. A resource is recommended for change only if
the optimal configuration is cheaper than the current one; infeasible instances
(demand exceeding the largest catalog type) are reported and skipped.

\section{Methodology}
\label{sec:method}

\subsection{Demand Forecasting}
The analyze stage forecasts the daily-cost series to estimate the base demand
$b_t+s_t$ that justifies reserved commitments. We use an ensemble of five models
behind a common \texttt{fit/predict} interface: \emph{Naive} (last value),
\emph{Seasonal Naive} (value seven days prior), \emph{ETS} (Holt-Winters with
additive weekly seasonality)~\cite{hyndman2021fpp},
\emph{Prophet}~\cite{taylor2018prophet}, and auto-tuned
\emph{SARIMA}~\cite{box2015timeseries}. Each model emits a point forecast and a
$95\%$ interval. Model choice is data-dependent: with little history a robust
baseline is preferred, and the system selects ETS once at least two months of
data are available (Section~\ref{sec:eval}).

\subsection{Surge Detection and Anomaly Filtering}
Before fitting, the series is cleaned of surges so that anomalous spikes do not
bias the base-demand estimate. We flag day $t$ as a surge if it deviates from
its trailing window by more than three standard deviations
(rolling $z$-score, window $30$) \emph{or} falls outside the interquartile
fence $[Q_1-1.5\,\text{IQR},\,Q_3+1.5\,\text{IQR}]$; the two detectors are
combined by union~\cite{lavin2015nab}. Flagged points are excluded from training
but retained for surge characterization, since they define the headroom the
optimizer must provision.

\subsection{Walk-Forward Model Selection}
Models are compared by expanding-window (walk-forward) cross-validation: from an
initial training window the model forecasts a fixed horizon, the window advances
by a fixed step, and a fresh model is refit each fold. We report mean absolute
percentage error (MAPE), root-mean-square error (RMSE), and mean absolute error
(MAE); MAPE masks zero-demand days. This procedure respects temporal order and
avoids look-ahead leakage.

\subsection{Reservation, Rules, and Cold-Start}
Three mechanisms turn forecasts into actions. (1)~\emph{Reservation}:
long-running resources with at least 60 days of steady utilization are
recommended for one-year reserved pricing---committed capacity for the base
demand. (2)~\emph{Right-sizing}: the MILP of Section~\ref{sec:formulation}
selects the cheapest surge-feasible configuration. (3)~\emph{Rules}: eight
threshold heuristics (Table~\ref{tab:rules}) cover services for which an LP is
unnecessary (storage class, capacity mode, idle deletion, etc.). For new users
with no telemetry, an LLM-assisted cold-start module
(Gemini 2.5 Flash~\cite{gemini2024}) maps a short questionnaire about expected
traffic pattern and availability to an initial architecture, addressing the
cold-start case of reservation planning. A final deduplication keeps, for each
resource, the single highest-saving recommendation.

\section{Experimental Evaluation}
\label{sec:eval}

\subsection{Dataset and Setup}
We evaluate on a twelve-month workload (365 daily-cost observations, July~2024--
June~2025) for a simulated mid-size SaaS tenant. The workload is constructed
from open production traces---the Bitbrains GWA-T-12 VM
utilization dataset~\cite{shen2015bitbrains} and the Numenta Anomaly
Benchmark~\cite{lavin2015nab}---combined with live AWS Pricing API data, so that
utilization shapes, anomalies, and prices are realistic. The tenant's mean cost
is \$74.64/day (min \$36.10, max \$232.52), about \$2{,}239/month. Forecasting
uses walk-forward CV; optimization uses the MILP and rules of
Sections~\ref{sec:formulation}--\ref{sec:method} with $\gamma=1.3$. All figures
and numbers are reproduced by \texttt{paper/make\_figures.py} against the
shipped database.

\subsection{Surge Characterization}
Fig.~\ref{fig:surges} shows the daily-cost signal with surges flagged by the
rolling-$z$-score detector. The series exhibits a stable base around the median
of \$70/day punctuated by sharp, short-lived spikes reaching \$232/day---the
intermittent random surges that motivate headroom-based provisioning. The
detector isolates these spikes cleanly, leaving a base series suitable for
reservation sizing.

\subsection{Forecasting Accuracy}
Table~\ref{tab:mape} and Fig.~\ref{fig:mape} report MAPE by horizon from
walk-forward CV. Prophet is most accurate at short horizons (MAPE $7.9\%$ at
7~days, $9.8\%$ at 14~days), while ETS dominates the medium range
(10--90~days), and the seasonal baseline becomes competitive only at very long
horizons where all models degrade. Because reservation decisions hinge on
near-term base demand, the short-horizon regime is the operative one, and there
the ensemble achieves single-digit error. Fig.~\ref{fig:forecast} shows a
representative 30-day holdout in which the deployed seasonal forecaster tracks
the base demand at $11.9\%$ MAPE while the $95\%$ interval contains the
realized surges.

\subsection{Optimization Savings}
Running the optimizer over the tenant yields 19 recommendations totaling
\textbf{\$590.40/month}, about $26\%$ of the \$2{,}239 monthly bill.
Fig.~\ref{fig:savings} and Table~\ref{tab:savings} break the savings down by
service and action. The two largest items are RDS right-sizing
(\$362.81/month combined; a production PostgreSQL instance running at a fraction
of its provisioned size is reduced, saving $93.3\%$ on that resource) and EC2
reservation (\$208.42/month from switching six steady, long-running instances
from on-demand to one-year reserved pricing at $\approx 37\%$ unit discount).
Smaller but safe wins---deleting unattached EBS volumes, upgrading gp2 to gp3,
replacing costly NAT data paths with VPC endpoints, tiering cold S3 objects, and
downsizing an over-allocated Lambda---account for the remainder. Every
recommendation carries a confidence label and a human-readable rationale, so an
operator can audit it before applying.

\subsection{Discussion and Threats to Validity}
The results confirm the central hypothesis: serving forecasted base demand with
reserved commitments while holding percentile-based headroom for surges recovers
roughly a quarter of the bill without risking surge SLAs. The dominant savings
come precisely from the two levers of the base/surge model---reservation and
surge-aware right-sizing---validating the framing borrowed
from~\cite{chen2024cost}. Two threats to validity should be noted. First, the
evaluation uses a workload synthesized from open traces rather than a live
production account; while utilization shapes and prices are realistic, absolute
savings will vary by tenant. Second, the forecasting accuracy is measured on the
cost signal of a single tenant; broader validation across heterogeneous accounts
is future work. Neither threat affects the system's mechanism, which is account-
and dataset-agnostic.

\section{Conclusion and Future Work}
\label{sec:conclusion}
We presented Smart Cloud Optimizer, a forecast-driven system for cloud cost
optimization under intermittent random demand surges. By decomposing demand into
a reservable base and a surge component, forecasting the base with an
anomaly-aware ensemble, and right-sizing with a surge-aware MILP, the system
recovers \$590.40/month ($\approx 26\%$) on a realistic twelve-month workload
while forecasting near-term demand at single-digit MAPE. The work operationalizes
the base/supplementary reservation theory of~\cite{chen2024cost} in a deployable
data pipeline. Future work includes incorporating spot instances and a
threshold-based renew/cancel policy for reservations, validating on live
multi-tenant accounts, and closing the loop by automatically applying and
monitoring the recommended actions.
