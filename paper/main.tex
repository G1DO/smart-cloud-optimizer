\begin{abstract}
\noindent
Enterprise cloud users face rapidly growing compute bills driven by
workloads that combine a stable, seasonal \emph{base} demand with
\emph{intermittent, random demand surges}. We study how such a user should
provision capacity across the on-demand, reserved, and spot purchasing
options offered by infrastructure-as-a-service providers, and we instantiate
the resulting policy in a working software system. On the modeling side, we
formulate the baseline reservation decision as a newsvendor problem and show
that the cost-minimizing reserved capacity equals the $\delta$-quantile of
the demand distribution, where $\delta$ is the reserved-instance discount;
the optimal reserve-versus-on-demand decision for a workload reduces to a
simple utilization threshold $u^\star = 1-\delta$, and intermittent surges
are best covered by \emph{supplementary} reservations only when the surge
time-fraction exceeds $1-\delta_s$. On the systems side, we present
\emph{Smart Cloud Optimizer}, which ingests provider telemetry, forecasts
demand with classical and machine-learning time-series models, and solves a
mixed-integer program for instance right-sizing, complemented by rule-based
and large-language-model recommendations. On a year-long, realistic
workload, exponential smoothing forecasts demand at $12.1\%$ MAPE; the
newsvendor policy attains its theoretical optimum (verified against
exhaustive search) and reduces cost by $26.7\%$--$50.0\%$ relative to pure
on-demand provisioning depending on the discount tier; and the end-to-end
system identifies \$590/month ($54.9\%$ of the addressable spend) in concrete
savings. Our results connect the operations-management theory of capacity
reservation to the engineering practice of cloud cost optimization, and show
that a widely used industry heuristic---reserve long-running
instances---is a special case of the optimal threshold policy.

\medskip
\noindent\textbf{Keywords:} cloud computing; capacity reservation;
newsvendor model; reserved instances; demand forecasting; mixed-integer
programming; cost optimization.
\end{abstract}

\section{Introduction}\label{sec:intro}
% =====================================================================
Public cloud spending has become one of the largest and fastest-growing line
items in enterprise IT budgets, and a substantial fraction of it is wasted on
idle or over-provisioned capacity. The discipline that has emerged to manage
this spend---\emph{FinOps}---emphasizes continuous visibility, right-sizing,
and judicious use of provider pricing options \citep{storment2023finops,
deochake2023cloud}. The central difficulty is that the economically optimal
purchasing decision depends jointly on (i) the shape and uncertainty of the
workload's demand and (ii) the rich menu of pricing mechanisms that providers
expose: pay-as-you-go \emph{on-demand} instances, deeply discounted
\emph{reserved} instances (RIs) that require a one- or three-year commitment,
and very cheap but interruptible \emph{spot} instances
\citep{armbrust2010view, kumar2018survey}.

A recurring structural feature of real workloads sharpens the problem.
Demand is rarely stationary: it typically consists of a predictable,
seasonal \emph{base} level punctuated by \emph{intermittent, random surges}
triggered by planned launches, marketing events, or exogenous traffic spikes
\citep{chen2024cost}. Reserving enough capacity to cover the surges leaves
expensive capacity idle most of the time; reserving only the base forces the
user onto premium on-demand pricing exactly when demand is highest. The user
must therefore decide \emph{how much} baseline capacity to reserve, and
\emph{how} to cover the residual surges.

In practice, this decision is usually made with crude heuristics. A common
rule of thumb, embedded in many cost-optimization tools, is ``reserve any
instance that has been running continuously for more than 60 days.'' Such
rules are easy to implement but disconnected from the underlying economics:
they ignore the discount rate, the demand distribution, and the
intermittency of surges. At the same time, a rigorous operations-management
literature on capacity reservation exists but is rarely connected to the
engineering systems that actually issue cloud purchasing recommendations
\citep{chen2024cost, qu2024cloud, chen2023cloud}. This paper bridges that
gap.

\paragraph{Contributions.} We make four contributions.
\begin{enumerate}[leftmargin=1.4em,itemsep=2pt]
\item \textbf{A capacity-reservation model for cloud workloads with
  intermittent surges.} We model a user who provisions a seasonal base plus
  random surges across reserved, on-demand, and spot options. We prove that
  the cost-minimizing baseline reservation follows a newsvendor (critical-
  fractile) solution: the optimal reserved level is the $\delta$-quantile of
  demand, where $\delta$ is the RI discount (\Cref{prop:newsvendor}).
\item \textbf{A threshold characterization that rationalizes industry
  practice.} We show that the optimal reserve-versus-on-demand decision for a
  workload is a utilization threshold, $u^\star = 1-\delta$
  (\Cref{prop:breakeven}), and that supplementary reservation of surge
  capacity is optimal only when the surge time-fraction exceeds $1-\delta_s$
  (\Cref{prop:surge}). The ubiquitous ``reserve long-running instances''
  heuristic is exactly the special case $u\to 1$.
\item \textbf{An integrated, end-to-end system.} We present \emph{Smart
  Cloud Optimizer}, which couples demand forecasting (five time-series
  models), a mixed-integer right-sizing program, rule-based service checks,
  and a large-language-model advisor, all over a unified telemetry store.
\item \textbf{A reproducible numerical study.} On a year-long, realistic
  workload exhibiting exactly the base-plus-surge structure our model
  assumes, we validate the theory (the closed-form reservation level matches
  exhaustive search to within \$0.03/day), quantify forecasting accuracy
  (best MAPE $12.1\%$), and report concrete end-to-end savings (\$590/month,
  $54.9\%$ of the addressable spend).
\end{enumerate}

\section{Related Work}\label{sec:related}
% =====================================================================
Our work draws on, and connects, several literatures.

\paragraph{Cloud cost optimization and pricing.}
The economics of elastic, pay-as-you-go computing were articulated early by
\citet{armbrust2010view}; the FinOps movement has since operationalized cost
governance in practice \citep{storment2023finops, deochake2023cloud}.
Provider pricing mechanisms---on-demand, reserved, and spot---and their
trade-offs have been surveyed extensively
\citep{kumar2018survey, mazrekaj2016pricing, sharma2012pricing}.
\citet{wang2013reserve} design online algorithms that dynamically mix
reserved and on-demand instances under time-varying demand; we instead
characterize the static-commitment optimum in closed form and embed it in a
forecasting-driven system.

\paragraph{Capacity provisioning under uncertainty.}
A rich body of work formulates cloud provisioning as optimization under
demand and price uncertainty. \citet{chaisiri2012optimization} propose the
canonical stochastic-programming model trading reserved against on-demand
capacity; \citet{bulbul2021multistage} extend this to multi-stage stochastic
programs, and \citet{mireslami2021dynamic} and \citet{furman2025optimal}
study dynamic and periodic time-varying demand. Relative to these, we obtain
an interpretable newsvendor characterization tailored to the base-plus-surge
structure and connect it directly to a deployed system.

\paragraph{Workload forecasting.}
Demand forecasting is the input to any provisioning policy.
\citet{masdari2020survey} survey and classify cloud workload forecasting
methods. Classical time-series approaches such as ARIMA remain strong
baselines \citep{calheiros2015workload}, while machine-learning methods---
neural networks \citep{kumar2018workload}, LSTMs \citep{song2018host}, and
probabilistic deep models such as DeepAR \citep{salinas2020deepar}---target
complex patterns. Our system implements a spectrum from naive baselines to
Prophet and auto-tuned SARIMAX, and---critically---feeds the resulting
predictive \emph{distribution} into the newsvendor reservation policy.

\paragraph{Newsvendor and reservation contracts.}
The newsvendor model is the workhorse for single-period capacity decisions
under uncertainty \citep{qin2011newsvendor}. Reservation contracts with later
exercise have long been studied in operations management---e.g.,
\citet{erkoc2005managing} on high-tech capacity expansion in the same journal
as our reference paper. Most directly, \citet{chen2024cost} study capacity
reservation for cloud workloads with intermittent random surges, deriving a
newsvendor-type capacity and a two-threshold purchase/renew/cancel policy,
and \citet{qu2024cloud} analyze long-run-average cloud cost with reserved and
on-demand capacity. \citet{chen2023cloud} survey cloud value chains from an
operations-management perspective. We adopt this modeling lens but
specialize it to a discount-parameterized closed form and pair it with a
working implementation.

\paragraph{Right-sizing and resource allocation.}
\citet{lin2013dynamic} give a foundational online treatment of dynamically
right-sizing data-center capacity; \citet{hadary2020protean} describe a
hyperscale VM-allocation system. Cost-optimal placement via integer
programming appears in \citet{vandenbossche2010cost}. Our right-sizing module
solves a mixed-integer program in this tradition, but as one component of a
larger forecast-then-reserve pipeline.

\section{Model}\label{sec:model}
% =====================================================================
We consider a single cloud user (a firm) that must serve a compute workload
over a planning horizon of $T$ periods (days, in our implementation). All
quantities are expressed in \emph{capacity units}; without loss of
generality we take the on-demand price as the numéraire, $p=1$, so that
demand and cost are measured on the same scale. \Cref{tab:notation}
summarizes notation.

\begin{table}[t]
\centering
\caption{Notation.}\label{tab:notation}
\small
\begin{tabular}{ll}

\midrule
$D_t$ & demand (required capacity) in period $t$, $t=1,\dots,T$ \\

$\delta\in(0,1)$ & reserved-instance discount; reserved price $r=(1-\delta)p$ \\
$\delta_s$ & spot discount; spot price $s=(1-\delta_s)p$, with $\delta_s>\delta$ \\
$\beta\in[0,1)$ & spot interruption (reclaim) probability \\
$Q\ge 0$ & reserved baseline capacity (decision variable) \\
$d_b$ & deterministic base demand level \\
$\rho\in[0,1]$ & long-run fraction of time the workload is in a surge state \\
$u\in[0,1]$ & utilization: fraction of the term a unit of demand is active \\

\bottomrule
\end{tabular}

\paragraph{Demand structure.}
Motivated by real workloads (\Cref{sec:numerical}), we model demand as a
stationary, seasonal \emph{base} component plus \emph{intermittent random
surges}:
\begin{equation}\label{eq:demand}
D_t \;=\; d_b + S_t\,M_t,
\end{equation}
where $S_t\in\{0,1\}$ indicates a surge in period $t$ and $M_t\ge 0$ is the
random surge magnitude. Surges are intermittent: $\Pr(S_t=1)$ is small and
surges occur in episodes separated by inter-surge periods. We let
$\rho=\lim_{T\to\infty}\frac1T\sum_t S_t$ denote the long-run surge
time-fraction. The base $d_b$ is predictable and seasonal (e.g., weekly),
whereas the magnitude $M_t$ is random.

\paragraph{Procurement options.}
In each period the user serves $D_t$ from three sources, mirroring the AWS
menu:
\begin{itemize}[leftmargin=1.4em,itemsep=1pt]
\item \textbf{Reserved instances} (base contracts): a committed quantity $Q$,
  paid at the discounted rate $r=(1-\delta)p$ \emph{every period regardless of
  use}.
\item \textbf{On-demand}: any quantity, paid at $p=1$ only for what is used,
  always available.
\item \textbf{Spot}: paid at $s=(1-\delta_s)p<r$ when available, but reclaimed
  with probability $\beta$, in which case the load falls back to on-demand.
\end{itemize}
Reserved capacity is consumed first (it is sunk), then spot (when permitted by
reliability requirements), then on-demand. The single-period cost of
committing $Q$ reserved units and covering the residual with on-demand is
\begin{equation}\label{eq:periodcost}
c_t(Q) \;=\; \underbrace{(1-\delta)\,Q}_{\text{reserved (always paid)}}
            \;+\; \underbrace{p\,\posp{D_t-Q}}_{\text{on-demand top-up}},
\qquad \posp{x}\equiv\max\{x,0\}.
\end{equation}

\begin{assumption}\label{ass:prices}
Prices are ordered $0<s<r<p$ with $r=(1-\delta)p$, $s=(1-\delta_s)p$, and
$0<\delta<\delta_s<1$. The horizon is long enough that the empirical demand
distribution $\hat F$ is a consistent estimate of $F$.
\end{assumption}

The user's problem is to choose the reservation level $Q$ (and, in the
extensions, supplementary and spot quantities) to minimize total cost
$C(Q)=\sum_{t=1}^{T} c_t(Q)$, or equivalently the expected per-period cost
$\E[c(Q)]=(1-\delta)Q + p\,\E\posp{D-Q}$.

\section{Optimal Capacity Reservation}\label{sec:analysis}
% =====================================================================
\subsection{Baseline reservation: a newsvendor solution}
We first solve for the cost-minimizing reserved level $Q$, taking the demand
distribution $F$ as given (it is supplied by the forecasting module of
\Cref{sec:system}).

\begin{proposition}[Optimal reserved capacity]\label{prop:newsvendor}
Under \Cref{ass:prices}, the expected per-period cost $\E[c(Q)] =
(1-\delta)Q + p\,\E\posp{D-Q}$ is convex in $Q$, and the unique
cost-minimizing reservation level is the $\delta$-quantile of demand,
\begin{equation}\label{eq:Qstar}
Q^\star \;=\; F^{-1}(\delta)
        \;=\; \inf\{q: F(q)\ge \delta\}.
\end{equation}
Equivalently, $Q^\star$ is the newsvendor solution with critical ratio
$\mathrm{CR}=\frac{C_u}{C_u+C_o}=\delta$, where the underage cost is
$C_u=p-r=\delta\,p$ (the on-demand premium paid when reserved capacity is
short) and the overage cost is $C_o=r=(1-\delta)\,p$ (the cost of an idle
reserved unit).
\end{proposition}

The intuition is transparent: a larger discount $\delta$ lowers the cost of
holding reserved capacity, so the user reserves a higher quantile of demand.
The result also delivers a clean comparative static.

\begin{corollary}[Discount monotonicity]\label{cor:monotone}
$Q^\star$ is nondecreasing in the discount $\delta$. The fraction of demand
periods fully covered by reserved capacity, $F(Q^\star)$, equals $\delta$.
\end{corollary}

Thus a firm should reserve \emph{more} capacity, covering a higher demand
quantile, precisely when reserved instances are more deeply discounted---a
conclusion that aligns with \citet{chen2024cost}, who find that firms buy
more supplementary capacity as the unit discount rate increases.

\subsection{When to reserve: a utilization threshold}
\Cref{prop:newsvendor} prices the \emph{quantity} of reservation. We now ask
the dual, per-workload question that cost-optimization tools actually face:
\emph{should a given workload be on a reserved or an on-demand plan?} Consider
a unit of demand that is active a fraction $u$ of the reservation term
(its \emph{utilization}). Reserving it costs $r=(1-\delta)p$ for the entire
term; serving it on-demand costs $p$ per active period, i.e., $pu$ in
expectation over the term.

\begin{proposition}[Reservation break-even]\label{prop:breakeven}
A unit of demand with utilization $u$ should be reserved if and only if
\begin{equation}\label{eq:breakeven}
u \;\ge\; u^\star \;=\; 1-\delta .
\end{equation}
At the real AWS discount tiers ($\delta=0.37$ for one-year and $\delta=0.60$
for three-year RIs), the break-even utilizations are $u^\star=0.63$ and
$u^\star=0.40$, respectively.
\end{proposition}

\Cref{prop:breakeven} rationalizes---and corrects---the industry heuristic.
The popular rule ``reserve an instance that has run continuously for $\ge 60$
days'' uses elapsed running time as a \emph{proxy} for high utilization. The
optimal rule replaces the proxy with the economically correct threshold
$u^\star=1-\delta$: an always-on workload ($u\to 1$) should always be reserved
(since $1>1-\delta$), but a workload active only, say, half the term should be
reserved only under the deeper three-year discount ($0.5\ge 0.40$) and
\emph{not} under the one-year discount ($0.5<0.63$). The 60-day rule is the
$u\to 1$ corner of \eqref{eq:breakeven}.

\subsection{Covering surges: supplementary reservation}
Surges occur only a fraction $\rho$ of the time (\Cref{eq:demand}). A unit of
\emph{surge} capacity therefore has utilization $u=\rho$. Applying
\Cref{prop:breakeven} with the supplementary-contract discount $\delta_s$
yields the surge-coverage policy.

\begin{proposition}[Supplementary reservation for surges]\label{prop:surge}
It is optimal to cover a unit of surge capacity with a supplementary
reservation (rather than on-demand) if and only if the surge time-fraction
satisfies
\begin{equation}\label{eq:surge}
\rho \;\ge\; 1-\delta_s .
\end{equation}
Consequently, longer and/or more frequent surges (larger $\rho$) make
supplementary reservation more attractive, and deeper supplementary discounts
(larger $\delta_s$) lower the surge-frequency threshold at which reservation
pays off.
\end{proposition}

This recovers, in a transparent first-order form, the comparative statics of
the richer dynamic model of \citet{chen2024cost}: supplementary reservation
becomes more valuable as average surge and inter-surge durations grow and as
the discount rate increases. The full dynamic problem additionally trades off
cancellation fees and renewal timing, leading to their two-threshold policy;
our static condition \eqref{eq:surge} is the break-even that such a policy
oscillates around.

\subsection{Spot capacity: an interruptible third tier}
Spot instances are cheaper still ($s<r$) but interruptible. Serving a unit of
load ``spot-first'' incurs cost $s$ with probability $1-\beta$ and falls back
to on-demand at cost $p$ with probability $\beta$.

\begin{proposition}[Spot dominance and the reliability cap]\label{prop:spot}
The expected unit cost of spot-first service is
$\bar c_{\mathrm{spot}}=(1-\beta)s+\beta p$, and
$\bar c_{\mathrm{spot}}<p$ for every $s<p$ and $\beta<1$. Hence spot weakly
dominates on-demand in expectation for any interruptible load. If a fraction
$\alpha$ of demand requires uninterrupted service (an availability SLA), then
spot may serve at most the interruptible fraction $1-\alpha$, and the
cost-minimizing layering is: reserved base $Q^\star$ (\Cref{prop:newsvendor}),
spot for interruptible residual demand, and on-demand for the
reliability-constrained remainder.
\end{proposition}

\Cref{prop:spot} formalizes why production systems should aggressively shift
\emph{interruptible} surge load to spot while protecting an SLA-critical core
with reserved and on-demand capacity.

\section{The Smart Cloud Optimizer System}\label{sec:system}
% =====================================================================
The policies of \Cref{sec:analysis} require two inputs that a real deployment
must produce: the demand distribution $F$ (and its forecast), and a mapping
from abstract ``capacity units'' to concrete instance types. \emph{Smart
Cloud Optimizer} supplies both. \Cref{fig:arch} sketches the architecture.

\subsection{Demand forecasting}\label{sec:forecast}
The forecasting module fits five models to each daily cost/demand series and
selects the best by cross-validated error:
(i) \emph{Naive} (last value);
(ii) \emph{Seasonal naive} with weekly period $m=7$;
(iii) \emph{ETS} (Holt--Winters) with additive trend and additive weekly
seasonality ($m=7$);
(iv) \emph{Prophet} with weekly and yearly seasonality; and
(v) \emph{SARIMAX} with auto-selected orders over a weekly season.
Model selection uses \emph{walk-forward} (expanding-window) cross-validation
with an initial training window of $60$ days, a forecast horizon of $14$
days, and a step of $7$ days, scored by MAPE, RMSE, and MAE. The selected
model produces both point forecasts and predictive intervals; the latter
yield the demand distribution $F$ consumed by \Cref{prop:newsvendor}. An
anomaly-detection routine (rolling $z$-score with window $30$ and threshold
$3\sigma$, combined with a $1.5\times$ IQR rule) flags surge days for
separate treatment under \Cref{prop:surge}.

\subsection{Right-sizing as a mixed-integer program}\label{sec:milp}
Given per-instance utilization telemetry, the right-sizing module selects, for
each running instance $i$ in the fleet $\mathcal{I}$, a target instance type
$j$ from a catalog $\mathcal{J}$ so as to minimize cost subject to meeting a
utilization-derived capacity requirement. Let $x_{ij}\in\{0,1\}$ indicate that
instance $i$ is mapped to type $j$, let $c_j$ be the (on-demand) monthly price
of type $j$, and let $v_j,m_j$ be its vCPU and memory capacities. The program
is
\begin{align}
\min_{x}\;\; & \sum_{i\in\mathcal I}\sum_{j\in\mathcal J} c_j\, x_{ij}
  \label{eq:milp-obj}\\
\text{s.t.}\;\; & \sum_{j\in\mathcal J} x_{ij}=1, && \forall i\in\mathcal I,
  \label{eq:milp-assign}\\
& \sum_{j\in\mathcal J} v_j\, x_{ij} \ge V_i, && \forall i\in\mathcal I,
  \label{eq:milp-cpu}\\
& \sum_{j\in\mathcal J} m_j\, x_{ij} \ge M_i, && \forall i\in\mathcal I
  \;(\text{when defined}),\label{eq:milp-mem}\\
& \sum_{i\in\mathcal I}\sum_{j\in\mathcal J} c_j\, x_{ij} \le B,
  && (\text{optional budget cap}),\label{eq:milp-budget}\\
& x_{ij}\in\{0,1\}. \nonumber
\end{align}
The capacity requirement is derived from observed load with a safety margin:
$V_i = \tfrac{\pi_i}{100}\, v_{c(i)}\, h$, where $\pi_i$ is the $95$th
percentile of instance $i$'s CPU utilization, $v_{c(i)}$ is its current vCPU
count, and $h=1.3$ is a $30\%$ headroom factor; $M_i$ is defined analogously
when a memory signal is available. Constraint \eqref{eq:milp-assign} assigns
exactly one type per instance, \eqref{eq:milp-cpu}--\eqref{eq:milp-mem} enforce
sufficient capacity, and \eqref{eq:milp-budget} optionally caps total spend.
The program is solved with the CBC branch-and-cut solver. Right-sizing
(choosing $j$) and the pricing-plan decision (reserved vs.\ on-demand, governed
by \Cref{prop:breakeven}) are complementary: the MILP fixes the resource, and
the threshold policy fixes its purchasing mode.

\subsection{Rule-based checks and an LLM advisor}\label{sec:rules}
Beyond compute right-sizing, ten heuristic checks detect service-specific
waste, e.g.\ switching long-running on-demand instances to reserved (the
empirical analogue of \Cref{prop:breakeven}), down-sizing over-provisioned
serverless memory, upgrading storage volume classes, deleting unattached or
idle resources, switching database capacity modes, and replacing costly
network gateways with endpoints. Each check emits a recommendation with an
estimated monthly saving and a confidence label, and duplicates across modules
are resolved by keeping the highest-saving recommendation per resource.
Finally, for \emph{greenfield} provisioning (no telemetry yet), a
large-language-model advisor maps a short natural-language requirements
profile to a concrete, cost-optimized architecture with a cost estimate.

\section{Numerical Study}\label{sec:numerical}
% =====================================================================
We evaluate the theory and the system on a year-long workload drawn from the
system's reference dataset (account \texttt{aws-SYNTHETIC-001}), comprising
daily costs and hourly utilization across eight EC2 instances and nine
auxiliary services. The dataset is synthetic but constructed to be realistic;
all numbers below are regenerated by \texttt{paper/numerical\_study.py}.

\subsection{Demand exhibits the base-plus-surge structure}
\Cref{fig:series} plots the daily demand series. Over $T=365$ days
(2024-07-01 to 2025-06-30) demand averages \$74.6/day (median \$76.0, std
\$22.8, coefficient of variation $0.31$) on a base level of \$64.7/day. It
displays pronounced \emph{weekly seasonality}---weekday mean \$84.4 versus
weekend mean \$50.1---and \emph{intermittent surges}: five days
($1.4\%$ of the horizon) exceed the surge threshold of \$121.7
(median $+\,2\sigma$), peaking at \$232.5, i.e.\ $3.06\times$ the median. This
is precisely the structure of \Cref{eq:demand}, and it validates the
modeling premise of \citet{chen2024cost} on concrete data.

\centering
\includegraphics[width=0.78\linewidth]{fig1_demand_series.pdf}
\caption{Daily demand over one year: a seasonal base (dashed) punctuated by
five intermittent surges (red).}\label{fig:series}
\end{figure}

\subsection{Forecasting accuracy}
\Cref{tab:forecast} reports walk-forward cross-validation over $42$ folds.
Seasonal models dominate the naive baseline by more than a factor of two in

\section{Managerial Insights}\label{sec:insights}
% =====================================================================
Our analysis and experiments yield several actionable insights.
\begin{enumerate}[leftmargin=1.4em,itemsep=2pt]
\item \textbf{Reserve to a demand quantile, not to the peak or the mean.} The
  cost-minimizing reserved capacity is the $\delta$-quantile of demand
  (\Cref{prop:newsvendor}). Reserving for the peak wastes $60$--$63\%$ of the
  achievable optimum in our data; reserving the mean is close but
  systematically sub-optimal.
\item \textbf{Reserve more when discounts are deeper.} Both the optimal
  reservation level and the realized savings increase with the discount
  $\delta$ (\Cref{cor:monotone}, \Cref{fig:sweep}). Three-year commitments are
  warranted for the stable base; shorter commitments suit volatile components.
\item \textbf{Replace the ``60-day'' heuristic with a utilization threshold.}
  A workload should be reserved exactly when its utilization exceeds
  $u^\star=1-\delta$ (\Cref{prop:breakeven})---$63\%$ for one-year and $40\%$
  for three-year RIs---rather than when it has merely been running for a fixed
  number of days.
\item \textbf{Match surge coverage to surge frequency.} Supplementary
  reservation of surge capacity pays off only when the surge time-fraction
  exceeds $1-\delta_s$ (\Cref{prop:surge}); rare, spiky surges should ride
  on-demand or interruptible spot capacity, not standing reservations.
\item \textbf{Forecast the distribution, then optimize.} Accurate seasonal
  forecasting (ETS at $12.1\%$ MAPE) is what makes the quantile in
  \eqref{eq:Qstar} trustworthy; the forecasting and reservation steps are
  complementary, not substitutes.
\end{enumerate}

% =====================================================================
\section{Conclusion}\label{sec:conclusion}
% =====================================================================
We connected the operations-management theory of capacity reservation to the
engineering practice of cloud cost optimization. Modeling a workload as a
seasonal base plus intermittent random surges, we derived a newsvendor
reservation level, a utilization-threshold purchasing rule that subsumes a
widespread industry heuristic, and surge- and spot-coverage conditions. We
embedded these policies in \emph{Smart Cloud Optimizer}, an end-to-end system
that forecasts demand, right-sizes via mixed-integer programming, and ranks
savings. On a realistic year-long workload the theory matched exhaustive
search, forecasting achieved $12.1\%$ MAPE, and the system surfaced
\$590/month ($54.9\%$) in savings.

Several limitations point to future work. Our evaluation uses a single
synthetic-but-realistic account; validating on production multi-account
telemetry is the natural next step. The reservation analysis is static given a
forecast distribution; integrating the dynamic purchase/renew/cancel
dynamics---including cancellation fees and a secondary reservation marketplace,
as in \citet{chen2024cost}---into the running system would close the loop
between theory and deployment. Finally, jointly optimizing right-sizing and
the pricing-plan mix in a single program, rather than sequentially, is a
promising extension.

\Cref{sec:numerical} are regenerated by \texttt{paper/numerical\_study.py}
against the project database.

\section{Proofs}\label{app:proofs}
% =====================================================================

\begin{proof}[Proof of \Cref{prop:newsvendor}]
Write $g(Q)=\E[c(Q)]=(1-\delta)Q + p\,\E\posp{D-Q}$ with $p=1$. The map
$Q\mapsto\posp{D-Q}=\max\{D-Q,0\}$ is convex for each realization of $D$, and
expectation preserves convexity; adding the linear term $(1-\delta)Q$ keeps
$g$ convex. For the derivative, since
$\frac{\partial}{\partial Q}\posp{D-Q} = -\1\{D>Q\}$ almost everywhere,
\[
g'(Q) = (1-\delta) - \Pr(D>Q) = (1-\delta) - \big(1-F(Q)\big) = F(Q)-\delta .
\]
$g'$ is nondecreasing (as $F$ is), confirming convexity, and the first-order
condition $g'(Q^\star)=0$ gives $F(Q^\star)=\delta$, i.e.\ $Q^\star=F^{-1}
(\delta)$. The newsvendor reading follows by identifying the underage cost
$C_u=p-r=\delta$ (extra cost per unit served on-demand instead of reserved)
and the overage cost $C_o=r=1-\delta$ (cost per idle reserved unit), whence
the critical ratio $C_u/(C_u+C_o)=\delta$.
\end{proof}

\begin{proof}[Proof of \Cref{cor:monotone}]
$F^{-1}$ is nondecreasing, so $Q^\star(\delta)=F^{-1}(\delta)$ is
nondecreasing in $\delta$. The coverage identity $F(Q^\star)=\delta$ is the
first-order condition from \Cref{prop:newsvendor}.
\end{proof}

\begin{proof}[Proof of \Cref{prop:breakeven}]
Over one reservation term, reserving a unit costs $r=(1-\delta)p$ with
certainty. Serving the same unit on-demand costs $p$ for each period it is
active; with utilization $u$ the expected term cost is $pu$. Reservation is
preferred iff $(1-\delta)p \le pu$, i.e.\ $u\ge 1-\delta$. Substituting
$\delta\in\{0.37,0.60\}$ gives $u^\star\in\{0.63,0.40\}$.
\end{proof}

\begin{proof}[Proof of \Cref{prop:surge}]
A unit of surge capacity is active exactly during surge periods, hence its
utilization is $u=\rho$. Applying \Cref{prop:breakeven} with the
supplementary discount $\delta_s$ gives the reservation condition
$\rho\ge 1-\delta_s$. Monotonicity in $\rho$ and $\delta_s$ is immediate.
\end{proof}

\begin{proof}[Proof of \Cref{prop:spot}]
With probability $1-\beta$ the spot request is served at cost $s$; with
probability $\beta$ it is reclaimed and re-served on-demand at cost $p$, so the
expected unit cost is $\bar c_{\mathrm{spot}}=(1-\beta)s+\beta p$. Then
$p-\bar c_{\mathrm{spot}}=(1-\beta)(p-s)>0$ whenever $s<p$ and $\beta<1$,
establishing dominance. Under an availability SLA requiring a fraction
$\alpha$ of demand to be uninterrupted, only the interruptible fraction
$1-\alpha$ is eligible for spot; minimizing cost subject to this eligibility
constraint and to reserved capacity being sunk yields the stated layering
(reserved $\to$ spot $\to$ on-demand).
\end{proof}
