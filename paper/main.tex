\begin{abstract}
Enterprise cloud expenditure continues to grow, and a large share of it is
wasted because provisioned capacity is poorly matched to a demand that combines
a roughly stationary \emph{base} with \emph{intermittent random surges} driven
by planned or unplanned events. A firm meets this demand through three pricing
instruments: cheap but rigid \emph{base contracts} (long-term reserved
instances), flexible \emph{supplementary contracts} (short or cancellable
reserved instances), and a punitive \emph{on-demand spillover} for any
uncovered load. Classical operations-research theory shows that the optimal
reservation level follows a newsvendor critical-fractile rule and that the
supplementary-capacity decision obeys a two-threshold $(s,S)$ policy. This paper
operationalizes that theory as a deployable, data-driven system,
\textbf{Smart Cloud Optimizer}, which (i)~collects cost and utilization
telemetry from Amazon Web Services, (ii)~estimates the demand distribution with
an anomaly-aware ensemble of five forecasting models, (iii)~sets reserved
capacity from the estimated critical fractile and sizes surge headroom at the
$95^{\text{th}}$ percentile of load, and (iv)~realizes the threshold policy
through a mixed-integer program and eight commitment rules. In a numerical study
on a twelve-month workload built from open production traces, the forecasting
ensemble attains a mean absolute percentage error of $7.9\%$ at a one-week
horizon, and the optimizer recovers \$590.40 of monthly cost ($\approx 26\%$ of
the bill) across 19 recommendations. The study confirms that pairing
distribution estimation with newsvendor reservation and threshold-based
supplementary capacity yields substantial, automated, and explainable savings.
\end{abstract}

\section{Introduction}
Worldwide public-cloud spending is projected to approach \$600 billion
annually~\cite{gartner2023cloud}, and industry surveys consistently report that
roughly a quarter to a third of it is wasted on idle or mispriced
resources~\cite{flexera2024}. The root cause is a mismatch between
\emph{provisioned} capacity and \emph{realized} demand. This mismatch is most
costly when demand is not stationary: a steady \emph{base} load is overlaid with
\emph{intermittent random surges}---marketing campaigns, product launches, viral
content, or batch jobs---whose timing and magnitude are uncertain.

An enterprise cloud user (the \emph{firm}) manages this demand through three
purchasing instruments~\cite{chen2024cost,awspricing}:
\begin{itemize}
\item \textbf{Base contracts}: reserved instances on the standard long contract
(typically one or three years), priced at a deep discount to on-demand, used to
cover the stationary base demand.
\item \textbf{Supplementary contracts}: additional reserved instances, of
standard or shorter length, deployed to cover surges; they may be cancelled for
a fee or allowed to expire.
\item \textbf{On-demand spillover}: any demand left uncovered spills onto
pay-as-you-go pricing---the flexible but punitive option.
\end{itemize}
The firm's problem is to choose the base capacity and a policy for purchasing,
renewing, and cancelling supplementary capacity so as to minimize expected total
cost. Chen, Lei, and Moinzadeh~\cite{chen2024cost} formalize this problem and
prove two structural results that organize the entire solution space: the
optimal capacity follows a \emph{newsvendor-type} critical fractile, and the
optimal supplementary-capacity policy is a \emph{two-threshold} $(s,S)$ policy
borrowed from inventory theory.

That analysis assumes the demand distribution is given in closed form. In
practice, a firm does not know its surge distribution \emph{a priori}; it must
estimate it from telemetry, and the estimate must drive the reservation decision
automatically across a heterogeneous fleet. This paper takes that
\emph{systems-and-data} step. We present Smart Cloud Optimizer, a deployed
platform that turns raw Amazon Web Services (AWS) billing and CloudWatch data
into ranked, explainable reservation and right-sizing actions, using the
newsvendor and threshold structure of~\cite{chen2024cost} as its decision
backbone.

\noindent\textbf{Contributions.}
\begin{itemize}
\item We cast a working cloud-cost system in the base/supplementary
reservation framework of~\cite{chen2024cost}, mapping reserved instances to base
contracts, surge headroom to supplementary capacity, and pay-as-you-go to
on-demand spillover (Sections~\ref{sec:model}--\ref{sec:policy}).
\item We replace the assumed demand distribution with a learned one: an
anomaly-aware ensemble of five forecasting models estimates the base demand and
the surge tail that feed the newsvendor critical fractile
(Section~\ref{sec:model}).
\item We realize the $(s,S)$ supplementary-capacity policy with a mixed-integer
program for right-sizing and eight commitment rules, sizing surge headroom at
the $95^{\text{th}}$ percentile of load (Section~\ref{sec:policy}).
\item We report a numerical study on a twelve-month workload built from open
production traces, measuring forecasting accuracy and \$590.40/month
($\approx 26\%$) of recoverable cost (Section~\ref{sec:numerical}).
\end{itemize}

The remainder of the paper follows the structure of the anchor work:
Section~\ref{sec:related} reviews the literature, Section~\ref{sec:model} states
the model and the newsvendor reservation result, Section~\ref{sec:policy}
develops the threshold policy and its data-driven realization,
Section~\ref{sec:numerical} presents the numerical study, and
Section~\ref{sec:conclusion} concludes.

\section{Literature Review}
\label{sec:related}
The reserved-versus-on-demand decision under demand uncertainty has been
attacked from three largely complementary directions.

\subsection{Stochastic and Robust Programming}
The canonical formulation is the broker's two-stage stochastic integer program
of Chaisiri \emph{et~al.}~\cite{chaisiri2012}: stage one commits to reservations
under demand and price uncertainty, stage two buys on-demand to cover the
realized shortfall, solved by sample-average approximation. Multi-stage
extensions model the rolling-horizon nature of reservations~\cite{hadji2021},
and robust optimization with a budget of uncertainty~\cite{bertsimas2004} is the
natural alternative when the distribution itself is unknown. These models scale
to many instance types but require the demand distribution as input and yield no
closed-form policy.

\subsection{Inventory- and Queueing-Theoretic Models}
The anchor paper~\cite{chen2024cost} sits in this tradition: its newsvendor and
$(s,S)$ results are direct adaptations of inventory
theory~\cite{porteus2002}. Furman and Diamant~\cite{furman2025} solve the
provider-side dual, optimal capacity planning under periodic time-varying
demand. The queueing strand models bursty arrivals with Markov-modulated Poisson
processes that fit real cloud traces well~\cite{casale2012}, producing
queue-threshold activation policies directly analogous to supplementary-capacity
activation. Our work is data-driven but keeps this theory as its skeleton.

\subsection{Learning-Based Methods}
A simpler pipeline forecasts demand and feeds the forecast to a reservation
optimizer. Strong workload predictors include ARIMA~\cite{calheiros2015workload},
Prophet~\cite{taylor2018prophet}, Holt-Winters/ETS~\cite{hyndman2021fpp}, and
transformer models~\cite{arbat2022}; large-provider studies predict VM behavior
at datacenter scale~\cite{cortez2017azure}. Reinforcement learning has been
applied widely to autoscaling~\cite{gari2021,lorido2014autoscaling,roy2011autoscaling},
though the long lease horizon of reservations complicates credit assignment. We
adopt the forecast-then-optimize pattern but, unlike pure autoscaling, target
the cost signal and feed it into the newsvendor reservation rule. The emerging
FinOps discipline~\cite{forsgren2020finops} frames the practical need our system
addresses.

\section{System Model and Newsvendor Reservation}
\label{sec:model}

\subsection{Architecture}
Smart Cloud Optimizer follows a three-stage pipeline---\emph{collect},
\emph{analyze}, \emph{optimize}---over a single multi-tenant store
(Fig.~\ref{fig:arch}). A collector assumes a cross-account IAM role via STS and
pulls twelve months of cost, utilization, inventory, and pricing data from AWS
Cost Explorer, CloudWatch, and the Pricing API for ten services; only the role
ARN is persisted, never long-lived secrets. Telemetry is normalized into 30
relational tables keyed by \texttt{(timestamp, user\_id, resource\_id)}; the
analyze and optimize stages read from this store, and a dashboard presents
ranked recommendations.

\subsection{Demand Model}
\label{sec:demandmodel}
Let $d_t$ denote realized demand (here, daily cost) on day $t$. Following the
base/surge decomposition of~\cite{chen2024cost}, we write
\begin{equation}
d_t = \underbrace{b_t}_{\text{base}} + \underbrace{s_t}_{\text{seasonal}}
      + \underbrace{u_t}_{\text{surge}} + \varepsilon_t,
\label{eq:demand}
\end{equation}
where $b_t$ is a slowly varying base level, $s_t$ a deterministic weekly
seasonal term, $u_t\ge 0$ an intermittent surge that is zero on most days, and
$\varepsilon_t$ noise. The base and seasonal parts are predictable and are
served by base contracts; the surge part is, by construction, hard to predict
and is served by supplementary capacity held as headroom. Unlike the anchor
model, the distribution of $d_t$ is \emph{not} given: it is estimated online by
the forecasting ensemble of Section~\ref{sec:forecasting}.

\subsection{Newsvendor Reservation}
Consider a unit of capacity over a commitment period. Let $c_r$ be the reserved
unit price and $c_o>c_r$ the on-demand unit price. Reserving one unit too many
wastes the reserved cost $c_o^{+}=c_r$ (overage); reserving one unit too few
forces on-demand spillover at premium $c_u=c_o-c_r$ (underage). The classical
newsvendor solution sets the reserved capacity $q^\star$ at the critical
fractile of the demand distribution $F$:
\begin{equation}
F(q^\star)=\frac{c_u}{c_u+c_o^{+}}=\frac{c_o-c_r}{c_o}
        =1-\frac{c_r}{c_o},\qquad
q^\star=F^{-1}\!\left(1-\frac{c_r}{c_o}\right).
\label{eq:newsvendor}
\end{equation}
The deeper the reservation discount $1-c_r/c_o$, the higher the fractile and the
more capacity the firm should commit---exactly the structural insight
of~\cite{chen2024cost}. Equation~\eqref{eq:newsvendor} is the bridge between the
theory and our system: the forecasting stage estimates $F$, and the optimization
stage commits capacity at the implied quantile. With the $\approx 37\%$ EC2
reservation discount observed in our data ($c_r/c_o\approx 0.63$), the base
contract is set near the $37^{\text{th}}$ percentile of demand and the residual
surge mass is covered by supplementary headroom, sized as in
Section~\ref{sec:rightsizing}. Table~\ref{tab:notation} summarizes the notation.

\section{Supplementary-Capacity Policy and Its Realization}
\label{sec:policy}
The anchor paper proves that the optimal management of supplementary contracts
is a two-threshold $(s,S)$ policy: when active supplementary capacity falls
below $s$, purchase up to $S$; expirations and cancellations are governed by
symmetric thresholds~\cite{chen2024cost,porteus2002}. Because the firm does not
know $F$ and operates over a heterogeneous fleet, we realize this policy with a
forecast-driven heuristic composed of three mechanisms.

\subsection{Demand Forecasting and Surge Detection}
\label{sec:forecasting}
The analyze stage estimates $b_t+s_t$ (the reservable base) and the surge tail
$u_t$ that determines headroom. We use an ensemble of five models behind a
common \texttt{fit/predict} interface: \emph{Naive}, \emph{Seasonal Naive},
\emph{ETS} (Holt-Winters, additive weekly seasonality)~\cite{hyndman2021fpp},
\emph{Prophet}~\cite{taylor2018prophet}, and auto-tuned
\emph{SARIMA}~\cite{box2015timeseries}, each emitting a point forecast and a
$95\%$ interval that empirically approximates $F$. Before fitting, surges are
removed so they do not bias the base estimate: day $t$ is flagged if it deviates
from its trailing 30-day window by more than three standard deviations
\emph{or} falls outside the interquartile fence
$[Q_1-1.5\,\text{IQR},\,Q_3+1.5\,\text{IQR}]$, the union of two
detectors~\cite{lavin2015nab}. Flagged points are excluded from training but
retained to characterize the headroom the policy must provision. Models are
selected by expanding-window (walk-forward) cross-validation reporting MAPE,
RMSE, and MAE.

\subsection{Right-Sizing as a Mixed-Integer Program}
\label{sec:rightsizing}
Given the estimated demand, right-sizing selects the cheapest surge-feasible
configuration per resource. For a fleet $\mathcal{I}$ with candidate catalog
$\mathcal{C}_i$, monthly hours $H$, and binary assignment
$x_{i,c}\in\{0,1\}$,
\begin{align}
\min_{x}\ & \sum_{i\in\mathcal{I}}\sum_{c\in\mathcal{C}_i}
   H\,\pi_{i,c}\,x_{i,c} \label{eq:obj}\\
\text{s.t.}\ & \sum_{c} x_{i,c}=1,\ \ \forall i; \quad
   \sum_{c}\text{vcpu}_c x_{i,c}\ge \rho^{\text{cpu}}_i,\ \ \forall i;
   \label{eq:assign}\\
 & \sum_{c}\text{mem}_c x_{i,c}\ge \rho^{\text{mem}}_i,\ \ \forall i; \quad
   \sum_{i}\sum_{c} H\pi_{i,c}x_{i,c}\le B, \label{eq:cap}
\end{align}
where $\pi_{i,c}=c_r$ if resource $i$ qualifies for a reservation (steady,
long-running) and $c_o$ otherwise, and $B$ is an optional budget. The headroom
encodes the supplementary capacity implied by the newsvendor fractile: rather
than the average (which hides spikes) or the maximum (an outlier), we size to
the $95^{\text{th}}$ percentile of utilization scaled by $\gamma$,
\begin{equation}
\rho^{\text{cpu}}_i=\frac{P_{95}(\text{CPU}_i)}{100}\cdot
   \text{vcpu}^{\text{cur}}_i\cdot\gamma,\qquad \gamma=1.3 .
\label{eq:p95}
\end{equation}
The factor $\gamma=1.3$ reserves $30\%$ as surge headroom---the data-driven
counterpart of a supplementary contract sized to absorb $u_t$ without paying for
it year-round. Program~\eqref{eq:obj}--\eqref{eq:cap} is solved per service with
the CBC solver via PuLP~\cite{forecastpkg}; a change is recommended only when the
optimum is cheaper than the incumbent, and infeasible instances are reported and
skipped.

\subsection{Commitment Rules as a Threshold Policy}
The $(s,S)$ thresholds are instantiated by eight rules (Table~\ref{tab:rules}).
The reservation rule is the policy's core: a resource that has run steadily for
at least $60$ days---the lower threshold $s$ on sustained utilization---is
promoted from on-demand to a one-year base contract, while right-sizing the
configuration down plays the role of cancelling or letting supplementary
capacity expire once load falls below the upper threshold $S$. The remaining
rules cover services for which an integer program is unnecessary. A final
deduplication keeps, per resource, the single highest-saving recommendation. For
new tenants with no telemetry, an LLM-assisted cold-start module
(Gemini~2.5 Flash~\cite{gemini2024}) maps a short questionnaire on expected
traffic pattern and availability to an initial architecture, addressing the
cold-start case of reservation planning.

\section{Numerical Study}
\label{sec:numerical}

\subsection{Dataset and Setup}
Mirroring the simulation-based evaluation of the anchor paper, we study a
twelve-month workload (365 daily-cost observations, July~2024--June~2025) for a
mid-size SaaS tenant. The workload is built from open production traces---the
Bitbrains GWA-T-12 VM utilization dataset~\cite{shen2015bitbrains} and the
Numenta Anomaly Benchmark~\cite{lavin2015nab}---combined with live AWS Pricing
API data, so utilization shapes, anomalies, and prices are realistic. The
tenant's mean cost is \$74.64/day (min \$36.10, max \$232.52), about
\$2{,}239/month. Forecasting uses walk-forward cross-validation; optimization
uses~\eqref{eq:obj}--\eqref{eq:p95} with $\gamma=1.3$. All figures and numbers
are reproduced by \texttt{paper/make\_figures.py} against the shipped database.

\subsection{Surge Characterization}
Fig.~\ref{fig:surges} shows the daily-cost signal with surges flagged by the
rolling detector. A stable base around the median of \$70/day is punctuated by
sharp, short-lived spikes reaching \$232/day---the intermittent random surges
that motivate the base/supplementary split. The detector isolates these spikes
cleanly, leaving a base series suitable for newsvendor reservation sizing.

\subsection{Demand Estimation Accuracy}
Table~\ref{tab:mape} and Fig.~\ref{fig:mape} report MAPE by horizon from
walk-forward cross-validation---the accuracy with which the system estimates the
distribution $F$ feeding~\eqref{eq:newsvendor}. Prophet is most accurate at
short horizons (MAPE $7.9\%$ at 7~days, $9.8\%$ at 14~days), ETS dominates the
medium range (10--90~days), and the seasonal baseline becomes competitive only
at very long horizons where all models degrade. Because reservation decisions
hinge on near-term base demand, the short-horizon regime is operative, and there
the ensemble achieves single-digit error. Fig.~\ref{fig:forecast} shows a
representative 30-day holdout in which the deployed forecaster tracks the base at
$11.9\%$ MAPE while the $95\%$ interval---the empirical $F$---brackets the
surges.

\subsection{Cost Reduction}
Applying the reservation and right-sizing policy to the tenant yields 19
recommendations totaling \textbf{\$590.40/month}, about $26\%$ of the \$2{,}239
bill. Fig.~\ref{fig:savings} and Table~\ref{tab:savings} break the savings down
by service and action. The two dominant levers are exactly those predicted by
the base/surge model: \emph{right-sizing} surge headroom (RDS, \$362.81/month
combined; a production PostgreSQL instance running far below its provisioned
size is reduced, saving $93.3\%$ on that resource) and \emph{base-contract
reservation} (EC2, \$208.42/month from promoting six steady, long-running
instances from on-demand to one-year reserved pricing at $\approx 37\%$
discount, consistent with the critical fractile of~\eqref{eq:newsvendor}).
Smaller, safe wins---deleting unattached storage, upgrading gp2 to gp3,
replacing costly NAT paths with VPC endpoints, tiering cold objects, and
downsizing an over-allocated function---account for the remainder. Each
recommendation carries a confidence label and a human-readable rationale, so an
operator can audit it before applying.

\subsection{Discussion and Threats to Validity}
The study confirms the central hypothesis: serving forecasted base demand with
reserved commitments while holding percentile-based headroom for surges recovers
roughly a quarter of the bill without risking surge service levels, and the
dominant savings come precisely from the two levers of the newsvendor/threshold
model. Two threats to validity follow those of the anchor work. First, like the
anchor paper's numerical study, our evaluation uses a workload synthesized from
open traces rather than a live production account; absolute savings will vary by
tenant. Second, accuracy is measured on a single tenant's cost signal; broader
validation is future work. Neither affects the mechanism, which is account- and
dataset-agnostic.

\section{Conclusion and Future Work}
\label{sec:conclusion}
We presented Smart Cloud Optimizer, a forecast-driven realization of the
capacity-reservation theory for intermittent random demand
surges~\cite{chen2024cost}. By decomposing demand into a reservable base and a
surge component, estimating the demand distribution with an anomaly-aware
ensemble, setting reserved capacity from the newsvendor critical fractile, and
realizing the $(s,S)$ supplementary-capacity policy through a mixed-integer
program and commitment rules, the system recovers \$590.40/month
($\approx 26\%$) on a realistic twelve-month workload while estimating near-term
demand at single-digit MAPE. Consistent with the open problems identified for
this line of work, future work includes adding a third \emph{spot}/preemptible
tier to the reservation portfolio, modelling \$/hour Savings-Plan commitments in
place of instance-count reservations, handling non-stationary (regime-switching)
demand with online change-point detection, and validating on live multi-tenant
accounts with automatic application and monitoring of the recommended actions.
